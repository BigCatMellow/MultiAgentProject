How to read this

Problems are sorted by severity. Critical means the story breaks without a fix. Structural means the story weakens significantly. Watch point means it's not broken yet but will become a problem at full draft. Each problem gets a plain diagnosis and a single question to drive the fix.
CRITICAL PROBLEMS

This matters because these two beats need to be load-bearing for each other. The sacrifice is the most costly thing Elizabeth has done. It should change the situation in a way that makes the next beat necessary. Right now it doesn't.

The question: What does the cookbook sacrifice break or remove that forces Pathwell to need Stansbury specifically now?
2. Diner → Museum is an "and then"

Elizabeth chooses to stay with Shade at the diner. Then she's at the museum. Nothing in between. The diner choice — "at least Shade is honest about what she is to him" — produces no consequence. It just suspends her until Pathwell shows up.

This is the flagged Act III blank. It's not a small gap. The diner and museum are the two most important beats in Elizabeth's arc in Act III, and right now they're connected by nothing. The diner choice needs to cause something that forces the museum confrontation.

The question: What does Elizabeth do or decide while with Shade that makes the museum scene inevitable rather than convenient?
3. "Why now?" has no answer for Stansbury's arrival

Even if you fix problem 1, Stansbury's arrival needs its own internal "why now." The notes say Pathwell "needs more help" — but why at this point in the story and not earlier or later? What changed that makes this the moment he goes to get Stansbury? Without an answer, Stansbury's arrival is writer-driven, not story-driven.

The question: What specific event or escalation immediately before the recruitment makes Pathwell decide he can't continue without Stansbury?
STRUCTURAL PROBLEMS
4. Pathwell and Stansbury are drunk for unspecified reasons

The crash scene depends on them being drunk. The drunk state is what makes Elizabeth furniture again, which is what makes her grab the wheel. But the notes flag this explicitly: they're drunk "because of something specific — establish the cause." It hasn't been established.

If they're drunk for a generic reason, the crash reads as convenient. If they're drunk because of something that happened in the story — a specific failure, a specific piece of news, a specific argument — then the crash is caused by a chain of events. The difference between those two versions is whether the midpoint feels earned.

The question: What happened that day that Pathwell and Stansbury would drink over, and how does Elizabeth know the reason while they're making decisions around her anyway?
5. Shade taking Elizabeth at the crash is passive for her

Shade casts protection and takes her. Elizabeth's role in this moment is zero. This is the second time in the story she's been physically moved somewhere by someone else (the first being Ch1). Both times are structurally intentional — she's being dragged. But the crash is supposed to be her biggest agency moment so far. Her grabbing the wheel is the peak. Then she immediately becomes cargo again.

The question isn't whether Shade takes her — he does. The question is whether there's even a small beat where she makes a choice in the immediate aftermath. Even a refusal. Even a word. Something that keeps her as a subject rather than an object at the moment of maximum consequence.

The question: Does Elizabeth make any choice in the moment Shade takes her, or is she unconscious/passive the entire time?
6. Camp comfort beat fails the removal test

The comfort-at-camp beat is supposed to establish Elizabeth's danger of becoming a passenger again. But the cookbook sacrifice happens whether or not she settles in comfortably. Mama Baga needs healing regardless. If the comfort beat doesn't actively threaten something that then gets disrupted, it's atmosphere, not structure.

Comfort also needs to be shown as a specific want, not a mood. What does Elizabeth want at camp that makes settling in dangerous? What is she on the verge of giving up without realizing it?

The question: What does Elizabeth almost decide to do at camp — some form of staying, stopping, or giving up on moving forward — that the cookbook sacrifice then makes impossible?
7. Shade portaling to camp after the museum has no interiority

"The draw compels him" explains the mechanic but not the character. Shade just had his plan fail — Elizabeth didn't stay with him. He's not getting the confession. He portals to camp. The notes say he's drawn toward confrontation, but the question is what Shade thinks he's doing in this moment. Is he retreating? Escalating? Does he know his plan failed? Does he tell himself a story about why this is still working?

Without interiority here, Shade arrives at camp as a plot device delivering himself to the climax. His death scene requires emotional groundwork. This is one of the places that groundwork needs to be laid.

The question: What does Shade believe he's doing when he portals to camp, and is he right?

The question: Does the reader understand during Shade's arc that he's wrong about what would release him, or does that only become clear in retrospect?
