# forbidden_patterns.md — Your Documented Habits (Never-Do / Watch-For)

*These are YOUR specific, repeated tendencies, pulled from problems actually flagged across this project — not generic writing advice. When revising, check against this list. Each entry says what the habit is and how to catch it.*

---

## PROSE-LEVEL HABITS

### 1. Exposition disguised as dialogue
Your most persistent habit. A character "explaining" something to another character that's really the author informing the reader.
**Catch it:** Does one character tell another something they'd both already know, or that exists mainly so the reader learns it? If yes, cut or convert to action.

### 2. Pathwell as docent / the magic lecture
The specific worst case of #1. Pathwell explaining how the world works to someone who just *experienced* it working. (The "Dewey Decimal" lecture; "magic is real and you're in a bookstore.") Both were killed in session — the pattern that produced them is the thing to watch.
**Catch it:** Any passage where a character narrates the rules of magic. The sugar-cookie/cookbook scene is the benchmark — magic revealed through sensory experience, never explanation. Write it like it's the sequel: characters treat the world as socially understood.

### 3. Filtering ("she saw," "she felt," "she heard")
Putting a pane of glass between the reader and the thing by routing it through a verb of perception.
**Catch it:** Search for *saw / felt / heard / noticed / watched*. "She saw her apartment light was on" → "Her apartment light was on." Cut the filter, render the thing.

### 4. Body-part cataloging
Listing multiple body parts instead of rendering one sensation deeply. (The bar hallway: "her elbows, wrists and knuckles all stretched out.")
**Catch it:** If a sentence names three body parts, cut to one and make it specific.

### 5. Metronome rhythm in narration
Your dialogue has music; your narration leans hard on subject-verb-plus-trailing-participle and flattens. ("She hurried to catch him, one block and she already felt lost.")
**Catch it:** Read narration aloud, separately from dialogue. If it has a sing-song sameness, vary sentence shape and length.

### 6. Narration without an opinion
The connective tissue between your (excellent) dialogue is competent but invisible — it moves bodies without voice. In Pratchett, half the wit lives in the *narration's* asides and attitude.
**Catch it:** Does the narration have a point of view, or is it just stage direction? At least sometimes, let the prose itself observe and judge.

### 7. Telling what the scene just showed
(Ch2: "This was all new for her" right after the missing-coffee-shop beat already showed it.)
**Catch it:** After an image or action does emotional work, delete the sentence that then names the feeling.

## STRUCTURAL / STORYTELLING HABITS

### 8. Adding volume when uncertain instead of cutting for clarity
When unsure, you add more rather than stripping back. Strip back and build forward.
**Catch it:** If a passage feels off and your instinct is to add explanation, try the opposite first — cut.

### 9. Elizabeth's passivity overstaying its purpose
Her passivity serves the early story; it becomes a problem when it extends past where it serves. She must be climbing the ladder, not drifting.
**Catch it:** In any scene past the opening, ask: is Elizabeth choosing, or being moved? If being moved, is that earning its place, or is it the habit?

### 10. Over-explaining / not trusting the reader
The umbrella over most of the above. Iceberg principle — show 1/8th.
**Catch it:** Anywhere you've explained a thing the reader could infer, cut it and trust them.

### 11. Don't let plot devices do character work
A device (the diary, the crash, a spell) should not stand in for a character making a choice. Feelings as fuel, not engines.
**Catch it:** Is this turn happening because a character *chose*, or because an object/mechanic made it happen? The choice has to be theirs.

## PROCESS HABITS (about how you work, not the prose)

### 12. Over-editing / pulling on resolved threads
You re-open settled decisions and rebuild from scratch each session, which has produced contradictions (the diary was simultaneously "kept the whole book" and "burned in the archive"). Named explicitly on June 8.
**Catch it:** Return to canon files as the single source of truth. If something's locked, build on it — don't re-derive it. Declaring Chapters 1–2 "locked" and not touching them is exactly why they escaped this.

### 13. Architectural symmetry that reads as designed, not lived
Three characters cleanly mapped to thematic positions + deliberate bookends can feel engineered. Not a current problem — a full-draft risk.
**Catch it:** Give each character at least one want that has nothing to do with the thesis. The diner especially is at risk of characters articulating theme too explicitly — keep it subtext.

### 14. Confirm new elements before building on them
(A process rule for collaboration, but also for solo drafting.) Don't treat an unconfirmed detail as established and build forward on it.
**Catch it:** If you're not sure a detail is locked, check the canon files before it becomes load-bearing.

---

## THE ONE-LINE TEST

Before keeping any paragraph: *Did I explain something the reader could feel? Did a thing move on its own that a person should have chosen? Could this be shorter?* If yes to any — fix that first.
